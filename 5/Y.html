Yattrium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: Y
  • Atomic Number: 39
  • Atomic Mass: 88.91 amu
  • Group: 3 (Transition Metals)
  • Period: 5
  • Block: d-block
  • Electron Configuration: (1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 4s2 3d10 4p6 5s2 4d1)
  • Valence Electrons: 2
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Silvery-white, metallic element.
  • Density: 4.47 g/cm³
  • Melting Point: 1526°C (2779°F)
  • Boiling Point: 3336°C (6037°F)
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Conductivity: Good conductor of heat and electricity.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Moderately reactive, reacts slowly with water to form yattrium hydroxide (Y(OH)3).
    • Forms yttrium oxide (Y2O3) when exposed to air.
    • Reacts with halogens to form yattrium halides.
  2. Corrosive Behavior: Forms a protective oxide layer in air, preventing further corrosion.
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Abundance in Earth's Crust: ~33 ppm (parts per million).
  2. Commonly found in rare-earth minerals such as:
    • Monazite
    • Xenotime
    • Bastnäsite
Uses of Yttrium:-
  1. Electronics: Used in phosphors for television screens, LEDs, and computer displays.
  2. Alloys: Improves the strength of aluminum and magnesium alloys.
  3. Lasers: Used in yttrium-aluminum garnet (YAG) lasers.
  4. Medical Applications: Yttrium-90 is used in cancer treatment for targeted radiation therapy.
  5. Ceramics: Used in high-temperature superconductors and advanced ceramics.
Biological Role:-
  1. Non-Essential Element: Yttrium has no known biological role in humans or animals.
  2. Toxicity: Inhalation of yttrium compounds may pose health risks, such as lung irritation.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. High-Temperature Stability: Yttrium oxide is stable at high temperatures, making it useful in ceramics.
  2. Flame Test: Produces a light green flame.
  3. Superconductivity: Yttrium compounds play a role in high-temperature superconductors.
History of Yttrium:-
  1. Discovery:
    • Discovered in 1794 by Finnish chemist Johan Gadolin in the mineral gadolinite.
    • Named after the village of Ytterby in Sweden, where gadolinite was found.
  2. 19th Century:
    • First isolated as a pure element in 1828 by Friedrich Wöhler.
  3. Modern Applications:
    • Found widespread use in electronics, lasers, and medical treatments.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Ytterby, Sweden, is the source of the names for four elements: yttrium, terbium, erbium, and ytterbium.
  2. Yttrium-90 is a critical isotope in cancer treatment.
  3. Yttrium is often grouped with rare earth elements, even though it is not a lanthanide.
