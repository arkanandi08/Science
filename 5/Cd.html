Cadmium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: Cd
  • Atomic Number: 48
  • Atomic Mass: 112.41 amu
  • Group: 12 (Transition Metals)
  • Period: 5
  • Block: d-block
  • Electron Configuration: (1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 3d10 4s2 4p6 4d10 5s2)
  • Valence Electrons: 2
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Soft, silvery-white, and metallic.
  • Density: 8.65 g/cm³
  • Melting Point: 321.07°C (609.93°F)
  • Boiling Point: 767°C (1413°F)
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Conductivity: Good conductor of electricity but not as high as silver or copper.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Moderately reactive, forming compounds with oxygen, sulfur, and halogens.
    • Stable in air but reacts with acids to produce hydrogen gas.
  2. Oxidation States: Commonly +2.
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Abundance in Earth's Crust: ~0.1 ppm (relatively rare).
  2. Found in nature primarily as a byproduct of zinc refining, commonly in minerals such as:
    • Greenockite (CdS)
    • Sphalerite (ZnS, with cadmium impurities)
Uses of Cadmium:-
  1. Rechargeable Batteries: Widely used in nickel-cadmium (NiCd) batteries.
  2. Electroplating: Used to coat metals to prevent corrosion.
  3. Pigments: Cadmium compounds (e.g., cadmium sulfide) are used in vibrant yellow, orange, and red pigments.
  4. Alloys: Used in low-melting-point alloys and solders.
  5. Nuclear Reactors: Cadmium rods are used as neutron absorbers in nuclear reactors.
Biological Role:-
  1. No Known Biological Role: Cadmium is not essential for life.
  2. Toxicity: Cadmium and its compounds are highly toxic and can cause severe damage to the lungs, kidneys, and bones if inhaled or ingested.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. Toxicity: Known for its significant environmental and health impacts when improperly handled.
  2. Soft Metal: Can be easily cut with a knife.
  3. Rare Use in Batteries: Although used in NiCd batteries, alternatives like lithium-ion batteries are reducing its demand.
History of Cadmium:-
  1. Discovery:
    • Discovered in 1817 by Friedrich Stromeyer, a German chemist.
    • Named after "cadmia," the Latin name for zinc carbonate, as it was found as an impurity in zinc ores.
  2. Industrial Use:
    • Became widely used in the 20th century for electroplating, pigments, and batteries.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Symbol Origin: "Cd" is derived from "cadmia," a historical name for zinc ores.
  2. Environmental Concern: Cadmium pollution is a major issue due to its toxicity and persistence in the environment.
  3. Softness: Cadmium is so soft that it can be scratched with a fingernail.
