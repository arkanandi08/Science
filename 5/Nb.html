Niobium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: Nb
  • Atomic Number: 41
  • Atomic Mass: 92.91 amu
  • Group: 5 (Transition Metals)
  • Period: 5
  • Block: d-block
  • Electron Configuration: (1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 3d10 4s2 4p6 4d4 5s1)
  • Valence Electrons: 5
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Shiny, silvery-gray, and soft metal.
  • Density: 8.57 g/cm³
  • Melting Point: 2477°C (4491°F)
  • Boiling Point: 4744°C (8571°F)
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Conductivity: Good conductor of heat and electricity.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Resistant to corrosion due to the formation of a protective oxide layer.
    • Reacts with halogens and strong acids.
    • Forms compounds such as niobium pentoxide (Nb2O5).
  2. Corrosive Behavior: Excellent resistance to alkalis, acids, and oxidation.
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Abundance in Earth's Crust: ~20 ppm (parts per million).
  2. Commonly found in minerals such as:
    • Pyrochlore
    • Columbite
Uses of Niobium:-
  1. Alloy Production: Added to steel to improve strength, flexibility, and corrosion resistance (used in pipelines, jet engines, and bridges).
  2. Superconductors: Niobium-based alloys are used in superconducting magnets for MRI machines and particle accelerators.
  3. Electronics: Used in capacitors and electronic components.
  4. Optics: Niobium compounds are used to produce special optical glasses.
  5. Jewelry: Niobium is hypoallergenic, making it suitable for body jewelry.
Biological Role:-
  1. Non-Essential Element: Niobium has no known biological role in humans or animals.
  2. Toxicity: Generally low toxicity, but fine niobium dust may cause lung irritation if inhaled.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. Superconductivity: Niobium is one of the key elements used in creating superconducting materials.
  2. Corrosion Resistance: Highly resistant to oxidation and corrosion, even in harsh environments.
  3. Hypoallergenic: Used in medical implants and body jewelry due to its biocompatibility.
History of Niobium:-
  1. Discovery:
    • Discovered in 1801 by English chemist Charles Hatchett in the mineral columbite.
    • Initially named "columbium" (Cb), but renamed "niobium" in 1949 to standardize its name.
  2. 19th–20th Century:
    • Primarily used in steelmaking and scientific research.
    • Developed for use in superconducting technologies and aerospace applications.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Name Origin: Named after Niobe, the daughter of Tantalus in Greek mythology, due to its chemical similarity to tantalum.
  2. Superconductor Role: Niobium-titanium alloys are essential in creating superconducting magnets.
  3. Confusion with Tantalum: Niobium was historically mistaken for tantalum due to their similar properties.
