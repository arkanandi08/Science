Strontium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: Sr
  • Atomic Number: 38
  • Atomic Mass: 87.62 amu
  • Group: 2 (Alkaline Earth Metals)
  • Period: 5
  • Block: s-block
  • Electron Configuration: (1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 4s2 3d10 4p6 5s2)
  • Valence Electrons: 2
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Soft, silvery metal that tarnishes quickly when exposed to air.
  • Density: 2.64 g/cm³
  • Melting Point: 777°C (1431°F)
  • Boiling Point: 1382°C (2520°F)
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Conductivity: Good conductor of heat and electricity.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Highly reactive, especially with water, forming strontium hydroxide (Sr(OH)2) and hydrogen gas.
    • Reacts with oxygen to form strontium oxide (SrO).
  2. Flame Color: Produces a bright red flame.
  3. Corrosive Behavior: Quickly oxidizes in air, forming a yellowish oxide layer.
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Abundance in Earth's Crust: ~370 ppm (parts per million).
  2. Commonly found in minerals such as:
    • Celestite (SrSO4)
    • Strontianite (SrCO3)
Uses of Strontium:-
  1. Pyrotechnics: Used in fireworks and flares to produce a bright red color.
  2. Ceramics and Glass: Used in cathode-ray tubes for televisions and in ceramic magnets.
  3. Medical Applications: Strontium isotopes (e.g., strontium-89) are used in treating bone cancer.
  4. Research: Radioactive isotope strontium-90 is used as a power source in remote applications.
  5. Metallurgy: Strontium is used as an alloying agent to improve the properties of aluminum.
Biological Role:-
  1. Non-Essential Element: Strontium has no known biological role in humans but is found in trace amounts in the human body.
  2. Bone Health: Strontium can replace calcium in bones to some extent, and strontium supplements are studied for potential bone-strengthening effects.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. Flame Test: Produces a bright red flame, making it useful in fireworks.
  2. Radioactive Isotope: Strontium-90, a byproduct of nuclear reactions, is hazardous due to its similarity to calcium and its tendency to accumulate in bones.
History of Strontium:-
  1. Discovery:
    • Discovered in 1790 by Adair Crawford, a Scottish chemist, in the mineral strontianite.
    • Named after the village of Strontian in Scotland, where the mineral was first found.
  2. 19th Century:
    • Isolated as a pure element in 1808 by Sir Humphry Davy using electrolysis.
  3. Modern Applications:
    • Widely used in pyrotechnics, electronics, and medical treatments.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Strontium compounds are responsible for the bright red colors in fireworks.
  2. Strontium is chemically similar to calcium and is often found in the same types of rocks and minerals.
  3. Radioactive strontium-90 is one of the most dangerous components of nuclear fallout.
