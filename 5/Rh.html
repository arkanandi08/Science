Rhodium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: Rh
  • Atomic Number: 45
  • Atomic Mass: 102.91 amu
  • Group: 9 (Transition Metals)
  • Period: 5
  • Block: d-block
  • Electron Configuration: (1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 3d10 4s2 4p6 4d8 5s1)
  • Valence Electrons: 9
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Silvery-white, reflective, and metallic.
  • Density: 12.41 g/cm³
  • Melting Point: 1964°C (3567°F)
  • Boiling Point: 3695°C (6683°F)
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Conductivity: Excellent conductor of electricity and heat.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Highly resistant to corrosion and oxidation, even at high temperatures.
    • Forms compounds with oxygen, halogens, and sulfur under specific conditions.
  2. Oxidation States: Commonly +1, +2, +3, and +4.
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Abundance in Earth's Crust: ~0.0002 ppm (extremely rare).
  2. Commonly found in ores with other platinum group metals, such as:
    • Nickel and copper sulfide deposits
    • Platinum ores
Uses of Rhodium:-
  1. Catalysts: Widely used as a catalyst in automotive catalytic converters to reduce harmful emissions.
  2. Jewelry: Used as a plating material to provide a reflective, corrosion-resistant finish on white gold and platinum jewelry.
  3. Electronics: Used in electrical contacts due to its durability and resistance to oxidation.
  4. Optics: Used in mirrors and optical instruments for its high reflectivity.
  5. Glass Manufacturing: Used in the production of fiberglass and flat-panel display glass.
Biological Role:-
  1. No Known Biological Role: Rhodium is not essential for life and has no known biological function.
  2. Toxicity: Rhodium compounds can be toxic if ingested or inhaled.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. Corrosion Resistance: One of the most corrosion-resistant metals, even under harsh conditions.
  2. High Reflectivity: Among the most reflective metals, often used in mirrors and jewelry.
  3. Rarity: One of the rarest elements in the Earth's crust.
History of Rhodium:-
  1. Discovery:
    • Discovered in 1803 by William Hyde Wollaston, a British chemist.
    • Named after the Greek word *rhodon*, meaning "rose," due to the rose-colored compounds it forms.
  2. Modern Applications:
    • Gained prominence in the 20th century with the development of catalytic converters.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Most Expensive Precious Metal: Rhodium is often more expensive than gold or platinum.
  2. Symbol of Wealth: Due to its rarity and high cost, it is sometimes used in luxury items.
  3. Highly Durable: Its hardness and corrosion resistance make it ideal for industrial and decorative applications.
