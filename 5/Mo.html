Molybdenum

General Information:-
  • Symbol: Mo
  • Atomic Number: 42
  • Atomic Mass: 95.95 amu
  • Group: 6 (Transition Metals)
  • Period: 5
  • Block: d-block
  • Electron Configuration: (1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 3d10 4s2 4p6 4d5 5s1)
  • Valence Electrons: 6
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Silvery-gray, shiny, and hard metal.
  • Density: 10.28 g/cm³
  • Melting Point: 2623°C (4753°F)
  • Boiling Point: 4639°C (8382°F)
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Conductivity: Excellent conductor of heat and electricity.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Resistant to corrosion and oxidation at normal temperatures.
    • Forms compounds with oxygen, sulfur, and halogens.
    • Common oxidation states: +2, +3, +4, +5, and +6 (most stable is +6).
  2. Oxidation: Reacts with oxygen to form molybdenum trioxide (MoO3).
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Abundance in Earth's Crust: ~1.2 ppm (parts per million).
  2. Commonly found in minerals such as:
    • Molibdenite (MoS2)
    • Wulfenite (PbMoO4)
    • Powellite (CaMoO4)
Uses of Molybdenum:-
  1. Alloy Production: Used in steel and superalloys to improve strength, hardness, and resistance to corrosion and high temperatures.
  2. Catalysts: Used in the petroleum industry for desulfurization processes.
  3. Electronics: Found in transistors, diodes, and electrical contacts due to its conductivity and heat resistance.
  4. Nuclear Industry: Used in reactors due to its high melting point and resistance to radiation damage.
  5. Lubricants: Molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) is a solid lubricant used in extreme conditions.
Biological Role:-
  1. Essential Trace Element: Molybdenum is vital for enzyme activity in plants, animals, and humans.
  2. Enzyme Cofactor: Found in enzymes like nitrogenase (in nitrogen fixation) and xanthine oxidase (in humans).
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. High-Temperature Strength: Molybdenum retains its strength at very high temperatures.
  2. Corrosion Resistance: Resists corrosion even in acidic and basic environments.
  3. Low Thermal Expansion: Its low thermal expansion makes it suitable for precision applications.
History of Molybdenum:-
  1. Discovery:
    • Identified as a distinct element in 1778 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele.
    • First isolated as a metal in 1781 by Peter Jacob Hjelm.
  2. Modern Applications:
    • Widely used in alloys, electronics, and catalysts since the 20th century.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Rare Metal: Although rare, molybdenum is crucial for high-performance alloys.
  2. Enzyme Importance: Essential for nitrogen fixation in plants, playing a key role in agriculture.
  3. High-Temperature Utility: Used in aerospace and industrial applications where extreme heat resistance is needed.
