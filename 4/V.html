Vanadium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: V
  • Atomic Number: 23
  • Atomic Mass: 50.94 amu
  • Group: 5 (Transition Metals)
  • Period: 4
  • Block: d-block
  • Electron Configuration: (1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 3d3 4s2)
  • Valence Electrons: 5
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Silvery-gray, ductile metal.
  • Density: 6.11 g/cm³.
  • Melting Point: 1,910°C (3,470°F).
  • Boiling Point: 3,407°C (6,165°F).
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid.
  • Conductivity: Good conductor of heat and electricity.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Forms a protective oxide layer that prevents further oxidation.
    • Reacts with oxygen, halogens, and acids at elevated temperatures.
  2. Oxidation States:
    • Exhibits multiple oxidation states (+2, +3, +4, +5), with +5 being the most stable.
  3. Compound Formation:
    • Forms compounds such as vanadium pentoxide (V2O5) and vanadium(III) chloride (VCl3).
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Abundance in Earth's Crust: ~0.019% (20th most abundant element).
  2. Common Sources:
    • Found in minerals such as vanadinite (Pb5(VO4)3Cl) and carnotite (K2UO2(VO4)2·3H2O).
    • Also recovered from fossil fuel deposits and slag from iron smelting.
Uses of Vanadium:-
  1. Steel Industry:
    • Used to produce high-strength, low-alloy steels for construction, pipelines, and tools.
  2. Energy Storage:
    • Used in vanadium redox flow batteries for large-scale energy storage.
  3. Catalysis:
    • Vanadium pentoxide (V2O5) is used as a catalyst in the production of sulfuric acid.
  4. Aerospace:
    • Used in titanium-aluminum-vanadium alloys for aerospace applications.
Biological Role:-
  1. Trace Element:
    • Vanadium is a trace element in some organisms, including humans, but its biological role is not well understood.
  2. Toxicity:
    • Vanadium compounds can be toxic at high concentrations.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. Multiple Oxidation States: Vanadium exhibits a wide range of oxidation states, making it versatile in chemical reactions.
  2. Corrosion Resistance: Forms a stable oxide layer that protects it from further corrosion.
  3. High Strength: Vanadium alloys are strong and lightweight, making them valuable in advanced industries.
History of Vanadium:-
  1. Discovery:
    • Discovered in 1801 by Andrés Manuel del Río, a Spanish-Mexican scientist, but was misidentified.
    • Rediscovered in 1830 by Nils Gabriel Sefström, a Swedish chemist.
  2. Naming:
    • Named "Vanadium" after Vanadis, the Scandinavian goddess of beauty, due to its colorful compounds.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Colorful Compounds: Vanadium compounds can exhibit a variety of bright colors, depending on their oxidation state.
  2. Space Exploration: Vanadium alloys are used in spacecraft due to their strength and resistance to high temperatures.
  3. Vanadium Batteries: Vanadium redox flow batteries are considered promising for renewable energy storage.
