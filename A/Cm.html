Curium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: Cm
  • Atomic Number: 96
  • Atomic Mass: 247 amu
  • Group: Actinides
  • Period: 7
  • Block: f-block
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f76d17s2
  • Valence Electrons: 3
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Silvery-white, metallic.
  • Density: 13.51 g/cm³
  • Melting Point: 1,340°C (2,444°F)
  • Boiling Point: Estimated around 3,110°C (5,630°F)
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid
  • Radioactive: Emits alpha radiation.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Reacts with oxygen to form curium oxide (CmO2).
    • Reacts with halogens to form curium halides.
  2. Oxidation States: +3, +4.
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Not found naturally; produced synthetically in nuclear reactors.
  2. Created by neutron bombardment of plutonium-239 or americium-241.
Uses of Curium:-
  1. Power Source: Used in radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) for space missions.
  2. Scientific Research: Helps in studying heavy elements and actinide chemistry.
Biological Role:-
  1. Highly radioactive and toxic.
  2. No known biological function.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. Man-Made Element: First synthesized in 1944.
  2. Named After: Marie and Pierre Curie.
  3. Highly Radioactive: Emits significant alpha radiation.
History of Curium:-
  1. Discovery: Discovered in 1944 by Glenn T. Seaborg and colleagues at UC Berkeley.
  2. Production: First created by bombarding plutonium-239 with alpha particles.
  3. Applications: Used in space exploration and scientific studies.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Curium-242 and Curium-244 are the most commonly used isotopes.
  2. Curium emits strong alpha radiation, making it a potential heat source.
  3. Its intense radioactivity limits its practical applications.
