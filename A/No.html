Nobelium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: No
  • Atomic Number: 102
  • Atomic Mass: [259] amu (most stable isotope)
  • Group: Actinides
  • Period: 7
  • Block: f-block
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f147s2
  • Valence Electrons: 2
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Unknown, but expected to be metallic.
  • Density: Unknown (predicted to be around 9.9 g/cm³).
  • Melting Point: ~827°C (1,521°F) (estimated).
  • Boiling Point: Unknown.
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid (predicted).
  • Radioactive: Highly radioactive with short-lived isotopes.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Reacts similarly to other actinides.
    • Forms stable No2+ and No3+ oxidation states in aqueous solutions.
  2. Oxidation States: +2, +3 (most stable is +2).
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Does not occur naturally; entirely synthetic.
  2. Produced in particle accelerators by bombarding curium-244 or berkelium-249 with carbon or oxygen ions.
Uses of Nobelium:-
  1. Scientific Research: Used only in atomic and nuclear research.
  2. Study of Actinide Chemistry: Helps understand heavy element behavior.
Biological Role:-
  1. Highly radioactive and toxic.
  2. No biological function.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. One of the few actinides where the +2 oxidation state is more stable than +3.
  2. Named after Alfred Nobel, the inventor of dynamite and founder of the Nobel Prize.
  3. One of the heaviest elements successfully studied chemically.
History of Nobelium:-
  1. Discovery: First claimed in 1957 by scientists at the Nobel Institute in Sweden, later confirmed by the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) in Russia and Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory in the U.S.
  2. Production: Created by bombarding curium-244 with carbon-12 ions.
  3. Recognition: Officially credited to the team at JINR in 1966.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Nobelium has no stable isotopes; its most stable isotope, No-259, has a half-life of about 58 minutes.
  2. It was the first transuranium element where the +2 oxidation state was found to be more stable than +3.
  3. The element's name was chosen to honor Alfred Nobel's contributions to science.
