Thorium

General Information:-
  • Symbol: Th
  • Atomic Number: 90
  • Atomic Mass: 232.04 amu
  • Group: Actinides
  • Period: 7
  • Block: f-block
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 6d27s2
  • Valence Electrons: 4
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Silvery-white, tarnishes in air.
  • Density: 11.72 g/cm³.
  • Melting Point: 1,750°C (3,182°F).
  • Boiling Point: 4,790°C (8,654°F).
  • State at Room Temperature: Solid.
  • Radioactive: Weakly radioactive with a very long half-life.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Slowly oxidizes in air, forming thorium dioxide (ThO2).
    • Reacts with water and acids, releasing hydrogen gas.
    • Common oxidation state: +4.
  2. Oxidation States: +4 (most stable), +3 (less common).
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. More abundant than uranium in Earth's crust.
  2. Commonly found in minerals such as monazite and thorite.
Uses of Thorium:-
  1. Nuclear Fuel: Potential alternative to uranium in nuclear reactors.
  2. Alloy Production: Used in magnesium alloys for aircraft engines.
  3. Gas Mantles: Formerly used in gas lamp mantles for bright light.
  4. Scientific Research: Used in radiation shielding and experiments.
Biological Role:-
  1. No biological function.
  2. Exposure to thorium dust or ingestion can be harmful due to radiation.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. Thorium-232 has a half-life of 14 billion years.
  2. Can be converted into uranium-233 for use in nuclear reactors.
  3. Highly resistant to corrosion.
History of Thorium:-
  1. Discovery: Discovered in 1828 by Jöns Jacob Berzelius.
  2. Name Origin: Named after Thor, the Norse god of thunder.
  3. Early Uses: Used in gas mantles for lighting before electricity became widespread.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Thorium reactors produce less nuclear waste than uranium reactors.
  2. Considered a safer alternative for nuclear energy due to its stability.
  3. One of the most abundant radioactive elements on Earth.
