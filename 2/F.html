Fluorine

General Information:-
  • Symbol: F
  • Atomic Number: 9
  • Atomic Mass: 18.998 amu
  • Group: 17 (Halogens)
  • Period: 2
  • Block: p-block
  • Electron Configuration: (1s2 2s2 2p5)
  • Valence Electrons: 7
Physical Properties:-
  • Appearance: Pale yellow gas.
  • Density: 1.696 g/L (at 0°C and 1 atm).
  • Melting Point: -219.67°C (-363.41°F).
  • Boiling Point: -188.11°C (-306.6°F).
  • State at Room Temperature: Gas.
  • Conductivity: Non-conductive.
Chemical Properties:-
  1. Reactivity:
    • Highly reactive and the most electronegative element.
    • Forms compounds with nearly all elements, including noble gases like xenon.
    • Reacts with hydrogen to form hydrogen fluoride (HF), a highly corrosive compound.
  2. Oxidizing Agent:
    • Fluorine is a powerful oxidizing agent, capable of oxidizing substances that other elements cannot.
Occurrence and Abundance:-
  1. Abundance in Earth's Crust: ~0.054% by mass.
  2. Found in:
    • Fluorite (CaF2), a major source of fluorine.
    • Cryolite (Na3AlF6), used in aluminum production.
Uses of Fluorine:-
  1. Industrial Uses:
    • In the production of fluorocarbons and Teflon (polytetrafluoroethylene, PTFE).
    • Used in manufacturing uranium hexafluoride (UF6) for nuclear fuel processing.
  2. Dental Health:
    • Fluoride compounds, such as sodium fluoride (NaF), are added to toothpaste and water supplies to prevent tooth decay.
  3. Etching Glass: Used in hydrofluoric acid for etching glass and metals.
  4. Refrigerants: Fluorine-based compounds are widely used in refrigeration systems (e.g., freons).
Biological Role:-
  1. Beneficial in Trace Amounts:
    • Fluoride strengthens tooth enamel and helps prevent cavities.
  2. High Toxicity:
    • Excessive fluoride exposure can cause fluorosis, leading to tooth and bone damage.
Unique Characteristics:-
  1. Most Electronegative: Fluorine has the highest electronegativity of all elements (3.98 on the Pauling scale).
  2. Highly Reactive: Can form compounds even with noble gases like xenon and krypton.
  3. Hydrofluoric Acid: One of the few acids that can dissolve glass.
History of Fluorine:-
  1. Discovery:
    • Isolated in 1886 by French chemist Henri Moissan through the electrolysis of potassium bifluoride (KHF2) and hydrogen fluoride (HF).
  2. Historical Use:
    • Fluorite (CaF2) was used in metallurgy since ancient times as a flux.
Interesting Facts:-
  1. Symbol Origin: "F" comes from the Latin word *fluere*, meaning "to flow," referring to its use as a flux in metalworking.
  2. Reactive Nature: Fluorine is so reactive it can ignite organic materials on contact.
  3. Unique Compounds: Teflon, a fluorine-containing polymer, is highly non-stick and resistant to chemical attack.
